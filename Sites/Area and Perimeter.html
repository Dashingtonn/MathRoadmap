Area and Perimeter

Here, you will learn about area and perimeter of various basic shapes, however most importantly you will be introduced to formulae/formulas in general, whose essentiality cannot be understated.

Unit square with area of 1.

When we look at the axes of the coordinate system, it's usually in steps of one, meaning that moving "once" to the right, for example, would move you one unit to the right. If your coordinate system is scaled then moving "once" to the right might move you two, five, or an arbitrary amount of units to the right. Unless specified otherwise, we always work with the coordinate system with a step of 1. Using this step of 1 we can draw a grid for the xy-plane, where one square of this grid is the unit square, a square with sides of length one and an area of one. Now we simply define all other areas as multiple of this "1" unit area.
Example: Find the area of the square with corners (vertices) (0 | 0), (0 | 2), (2 | 2), (2 | 0).
Solution: First, let us plot these points and connect them to draw the square. Because of the grid, we could simply count how many of those unit squares fit into this shape, which is four, however notice that we can also split this shape up into multiples of the unit square. First of all, the bottom half of this square is simply twice the unit square along the x direction (\(2 \cdot 1 = 2\)). Then, the whole square is twice this half, which is already twice the unit square.
Therefore: \(A = 2 \cdot 2 = 4\).
This sort of argument works for squares of any arbitrary side length. Let's use letters do denote sides, so for example, say we have a square with side length s (for side). s can be any length, be it 1, 2, 100 or 0.01. Therefore, the area "A" of a square with a side length of s is equal to s multiplied by s, which is the same as the s squared, so: \(A = s^2\).
Example: A square has a side length of 5. Find its area.
Solution: The area of a square is \(A = s^2\). In our case, s = 5, so we simply plug it into the formula by replacing s with 5. \(A = s^2 = (5)^2 = 25\). Therefore, the area of our square is 25.
Importantly, note that we don't need to use "s" to denote the length of a side of a square. We could also use "a" or "x" or any other letter or symbol we like. What is important is that a formula is correct when applied correctly. Crucially you must understand what a formula means and why it is the way it is, because then "remembering maths" isn't a thing, since you can logically derive these formulas by yourself, if you understand them, of course. Also, depending on the context the same letter can represent different things, as you will learn soon enough, which makes it even more essential that you understand formulae, not memorize.

The best way to demonstrate this, is for you to think about what the formula for a rectangle would be. We have already done this prior, when we halved the square and found the area of that bottom half, which is a rectangle of length two units and width one unit.
Example: Draw a rectangle with length 5 and width 3 on the cartesian plane and find its area.
Solution: Once again, we interpret this area as multiples of the unit square. We could count the amount of squares too but that's more tedious. The bottom third of the rectangle is five times the unit square along the x-axis. Then the total area is three times this bottom third, or: \(A = 5 \cdot 3 = 15\). Once again, we generalize this to a formula. For any rectangle with length \(\ell\) and width \(w\) the area \(A = \ell \cdot w\).

Perimeter

Square with sidelength s and rectangle with length \(\ell\) and width w.

The perimeter, or circumference, describes the length of the outline of some shape. The term perimeter applies to all shapes, while we use the term circumference for circular/elliptical shapes.
Since all the sides of a square are the same length, the perimeter is simply the sum of those sides, or, for a square with perimeter P and sides of length s, \(P = 4s\).
Example: Draw the square with side length 3 and find the length of its perimeter as well as its area.
Solution: For a square with side length s, the area \(A = s^2\) and the perimeter is \(P = 4s\). In our case s = 3, so we just plug that into our formulae:
\(A = (3)^2 = 9\), \(P = 4\cdot (3) = 12\).
For a rectangle, once again, the sum of the sides is the perimeter. Since it has two sets of two equal sides, \(\ell\) and w, then the perimeter of a rectangle is:
\(P = \ell + \ell + w + w = 2\ell + 2w\).
Example: A rectangle has vertices (1 | 1), (1 | 3), (4 | 3), (4 | 1). Draw it, find its area and perimeter.
Solution: The length of the rectangle, we can read off, is 3, or we can compute, that it's the right most vertex minus the left most vertex, which is 4 - 1 = 3. The width of the rectangle is 3 - 1 = 2.
Therefore, the area is \(A = \ell \cdot w = (3) \cdot (2) = 6\). The perimeter is \(P = 2\ell + 2w = 2(3) + 2(2) = 6 + 4 = 10\).

More shapes and their areas as well as perimeters will be studied next. For now, get intimately comfortable with area and perimeter. One thing that might be confused is how to work with units, as area isn't given in unit lengths but in terms of unit squares, which is the unit length squared. Think about it. We can define a unit length to be of length 1, then all other lengths, such as side lengths or perimeters are multiples of this unit length. The same with a unit square, all other areas are multiples of the unit area.
