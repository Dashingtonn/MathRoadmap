The Number Line

The Number Line.

The number line is a way to represent numbers and calculations and gives an intuitive understanding of how and why numbers act the way they do. It is simply a line with all real numbers on them from negative infinity to positive infinity, with the origin of this line at 0. Let's examine most of what we have learned up to this point and interpret it on the number line. Notice that moving to the right on the number line will increase the number, while moving to the left decreases the number we're on. Therefore, we can interpret addition as moving to the right on the number line and subtraction as moving to the left on the number line.
The number line also gives us an intuitive understanding of the fact that (-1)(-1) = 1. Observe what happens when we multiply something with -1, that is the sign of the number changes. For example, \(3 \cdot (-1) = -3\), which is equivalent to rotating about the number line by 180°, or half a turn. Therefore, a multiplication of two negative numbers represents two half rotations, which is a full rotation, or 360°, which doesn't change anything about the sign of the number. Equivalently, (-1)(-1) represents a start at 1 on the number line, followed by a half rotation to get -1, followed by a half rotation because of the multiplication with -1, which gets us back to our original position, that is 1.

Let's learn about a few more operations that can be applied to and between numbers and interpret them on the number line. We can also compare two (or more) numbers based on which one is larger. This should be easy enough to understand intuitively. ">" (greater than) denotes that the number left of the symbol is greater than the number right of the symbol. For example 5 > 3. If we want to show that 3 is smaller than 5 for example then we just turn the greater than symbol around, to get "<" (less than). For example 3 < 5. Now let us interpret/define these operations on the number line.
One number is greater than a second number if it is to the right of the second number. For example 4 > 2, because it is right of 2 on the number line. The "less than" operator works in the opposite way, meaning that, for example, -3 < 1, because -3 is left of 1. And lastly let me introduce a last operation, that is the absolute value. The absolute value, denoted by two bars (||) left and right of a number or expression, gives you the distance from the origin, so the distance from 0, on the number line. Examples: |5| = 5, |-3| = 3, |-10| = 10, |0| = 0. On a silly note from advanced mathematics, the actual definition for the absolute value is the distance from 0, the same definition we just went over.
