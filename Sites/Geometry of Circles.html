Geometry of a Circle

Types of lines that a circle has.

As described previously, a circle is a shape with a circumference and an origin, such that every straight line from the origin to the circumference has the same length. This line is also called the radius. A line which has its endpoints on the circumference of a circle and which passes through the center of the circle is called the diameter, and the diameter is twice the size of the radius. Other lines which terminate on the circumference but don't go through the origin are called chords. Infinite lines which intersect the circle twice are called secant lines and infinite lines which only intersect the circle at one point on its circumference are called tangent lines. On the right you see a diagram showcasing all of these types of lines.
Just like with the previous quadrilateral figures we studied, we also want to find the circumference and area of this circle, however that seems like quite the impossible task. How are we supposed to figure out how many of the unit squares fit into a circle? It turns out that there is a special constant that comes from circles. No matter how large or small a circle is, there is a special quantity that is the same for all of them, that is the circumference C divided by the diameter d, so \(\frac{C}{d}\). This ratio is the same for all circles, so we gave it a greek letter, "pi", which has this value: \(\pi = \frac{C}{d} = 3.1415926535...\) . Pi is a real number, because it can't be written as a fraction; it is irrational. Therefore, by definition, the circumference C of a circle with diameter d is: \(C = \pi d\). When working with circles, we rarely work with the diameter, instead the radius, so we write all of our formulae using the radius. The diameter is twice the radius so: \(C = 2\pi r\).
Example: A circle has a radius of 5cm. Find the circumference of the circle.
Solution: \(C = 2\pi(5cm) = 10\pi cm \approx 31.42cm\).
If you want exact solutions you will have to write your answer in terms of a multiple of pi (which is fine in most cases), otherwise, if you want an approximate answer, round to two decimal places.
As for the area of a circle, we will prove the formula for it using the method of exhaustion.

The Area of a Circle

Formula for the Area of a Circle via method of exhaustion

Cut up a circle into, say, 8 sectors, line half of them up with their tip facing down and half of them facing up to form a shape that is close to a rectangle. Obviously, this shape isn't exactly a rectangle, but it is approximately. To make this approximation more accurate we must cut up the circle into more parts. The more and more cuts we make the more it becomes a rectangle with a length of half the circumference and a height of the radius, so just by rearranging the circle we've turned it into rectangle whose area we can find easily. Since the length is half the circumference it's \(\ell = \pi r\), while the width is simply r. Therefore, the area of the rectangle which is also the area of the circle is \(A = \pi r^2\).
Example 2: Find the area of a circle of radius 3mm.
Solution: \(A = \pi \cdot (3mm)^2 = 9\pi \: mm^2\).
Example 3: Find the area and circumference of a circle of radius 5dm. Write your answer in m/\(m^2\) as a multiple of pi as well as an approximation.
Solution: \(A = \pi (5dm)^2 = 25\pi \: dm^2 = 25\pi \: dm^2 = 25\pi \: (10^{-1} m)^2 = 0.25\pi \: m^2 \approx 0.7854 m^2\).
\(P = 2\pi \: (5dm) = 10\pi \: dm = \pi \: m \approx 3.14 m\). We might also want to find the area of a half or quarter circle, or any general sector of a circle. The expression: \(\frac{\theta}{360°}\), where the letter in the numerator is the greek letter "theta", gives you a section of a circle, so for 180°, that expression would come out to 1/2, meaning that half a rotation gives you half a circle. Therefore, \(A = \frac{\theta}{360°}\pi r^2\) gives you the area for a circle sector of an angle theta.
Example 4: Find the area of a half circle with radius 2m.
Solution: Half a rotation is 180°, therefore \(A = \frac{180°}{360°} \pi (2m)^2 = \frac{1}{2}\pi \: 4m^2 = 2\pi \: m^2\).
Example 5: Find the area of a quarter circle with radius of 45mm. Write it in square centimeters.
Solution: A quarter rotation is 90°, so \(A = \frac{90°}{360°} \pi (45mm)^2 = \frac{1}{4} 2025 \pi \: mm^2 = 506.25 \pi \: mm^2 = 5.06\pi \: cm^2 \approx 15.9 cm^2\).
